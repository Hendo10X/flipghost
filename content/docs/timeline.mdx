---
title: The timeline
description: Adding, duplicating, reordering, and playing back frames.
---

The timeline runs along the bottom of the workshop. The strip of thumbnails is
your animation in order, left to right, and the controls above it decide how it
plays.

## Frames

Click any thumbnail to jump to that frame. The counter to the right of the play
button tells you where you are.

| Action | How |
| --- | --- |
| Add a blank frame | The dashed button at the end of the strip, or <kbd>N</kbd> |
| Duplicate the current frame | The duplicate button, top right of the timeline |
| Delete the current frame | The bin button, top right of the timeline |
| Previous frame | <kbd>←</kbd> |
| Next frame | <kbd>→</kbd> |

New and duplicated frames are inserted directly after the current one, not at
the end, so you can work into the middle of a sequence without shuffling
anything afterwards.

### Duplicate, do not add

Most of the time you want duplicate rather than add. Animation is mostly small
changes to the frame before, so starting from a copy and altering it is faster
than redrawing from nothing, and it keeps the parts that should not move from
drifting around.

Reach for a blank frame when the next drawing genuinely shares nothing with the
last one, such as a hard cut.

<Callout type="warn">
  Deleting a frame cannot be undone. <kbd>Ctrl</kbd> <kbd>Z</kbd> only covers
  strokes within a frame, so check the thumbnail before you press the bin.
</Callout>

## Reordering

Drag a thumbnail along the strip to move it. The frame you are hovering shows a
ring to mark where it will land. Release to drop.

## Playback

<kbd>Space</kbd> plays and pauses. Playback loops until you stop it, which is
the point: a loop that reads well on repeat is the goal, and watching it once
tells you very little.

Onion skins are hidden during playback, so what you see is what you will
export.

Pausing returns you to the frame you were on when you pressed play, rather than
leaving you wherever the playhead stopped.

## Frame rate

Three rates are available: 12, 24, and 30 frames per second. This is a property
of the whole animation, not of individual frames.

| Rate | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| 12 | The traditional choice for hand drawn animation. Half as many drawings, and it still reads as motion. Start here. |
| 24 | Film rate. Smoother, and twice the work. |
| 30 | Video rate. Smoothest, and the most drawings. |

Twelve is where almost everyone should start. Hand drawn animation has run at
twelve for a century because the eye fills in the gaps, and because drawing
thirty frames per second of finished work is how projects die.

The rate changes how fast your existing frames play. It does not add or remove
drawings, so moving from 12 to 24 will run your loop twice as fast rather than
making it smoother.
